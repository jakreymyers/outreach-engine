# Conversation Transcript: Maya + Sarah (u/exhaustedbutlifting)

---

## Pre-Call Prep

**Participant:** Sarah (u/exhaustedbutlifting)
**Date:** January 7
**Duration:** 23 minutes (planned for 15)
**Platform:** Zoom

**What Maya knew about Sarah:**
- Works in healthcare (night shift nurse based on posts)
- Lifts weights, been training 2+ years
- Posted about energy fluctuations affecting workouts
- Comment: "Some days I walk into the gym and just... can't"
- Active poster, thoughtful responses

**What Maya wanted to learn:**
1. What does "exhausted but still trying" actually look like?
2. What has she tried to solve the consistency problem?
3. What would make her switch from current approach?
4. Would she pay for a solution?

**Opening line prepared:**

## Transcript

*[Call starts, brief hellos and "can you hear me okay"]*

**Maya:** Thanks so much for taking the time. Your comment about walking into the gym and just... not being able to do it really hit home. I've been there. Can you tell me more about what that looks like for you?

**Sarah:** Oh god, yeah. So I'm a night shift nurse, right? And I try to work out after my shift before I sleep, but some days my body is just... done. Like, I'll get to the gym, look at my program - it says "5x5 squats" - and I just think, there's no way. Not today.

**Maya:** And what do you do when that happens?

**Sarah:** Honestly? Sometimes I just leave. Which feels terrible. Other times I'll do like... a sad version of the workout. Like, I'll do the squats but with half the weight and half the sets. And then I feel like I failed anyway.

**Maya:** *[Note: "feel like I failed anyway" - key quote]* Why does it feel like failing?

**Sarah:** Because the program says 5x5. If I do 3x3 at lighter weight, I didn't follow the program. And then part of me is like, "well, if you're not going to do it right, why bother?"

**Maya:** That all-or-nothing thing.

**Sarah:** Exactly. Which is dumb because logically I know SOMETHING is better than nothing. But the app doesn't tell me that. It just shows a big red X next to my workout.

**Maya:** *[Note: "big red X" - emotional detail]* What app are you using?

**Sarah:** Strong app. Which is actually great for tracking, but it's just a tracker. There's no... intelligence to it, I guess? It doesn't know I worked a 12-hour shift.

**Maya:** Have you tried any apps that adapt? Like, some of the AI ones that are out there?

**Sarah:** I tried Fitbod for a while. It does adjust based on what equipment you have, but not based on how you feel. And honestly, it felt kind of random. Like, I didn't trust the workouts it was giving me.

**Maya:** *[Note: Trust issue with AI recommendations]* What would make you trust it more?

**Sarah:** Hmm. I think... if I felt like it understood my goals? Fitbod felt like it was just generating random workouts. I want something that knows I'm trying to build strength in X, Y, Z, and adjusts accordingly.

**Maya:** Got it. So it's not just "adapt to today" but "adapt while keeping me on track toward something."

**Sarah:** Yes! That's exactly it. I don't want permission to be lazy. I want... intelligent flexibility, I guess?

**Maya:** *[Note: "intelligent flexibility" - her words for the product]* Let me ask this differently. If you woke up tomorrow and this problem was just... solved. What would be different?

**Sarah:** *[Pauses]* I think I'd actually go to the gym more. Because right now, if I'm not feeling it, I skip. If I had something that said "okay, here's what you CAN do today that still moves you forward" - I'd probably go.

**Maya:** How often do you skip now?

**Sarah:** Maybe once a week? Sometimes twice if it's a rough week at work.

**Maya:** And if this magical solution existed, how often do you think you'd skip?

**Sarah:** Probably never? Or like, only if I was actually sick. Because there'd always be SOMETHING I could do.

**Maya:** *[Note: Strong signal on the value prop]* Okay, I have to ask the uncomfortable question. Would you pay for something like this?

**Sarah:** *[Laughs]* I was wondering when you'd ask that.

**Maya:** Full disclosure - I'm not selling anything. I don't have anything to sell yet. I'm genuinely trying to figure out if this is worth building.

**Sarah:** I appreciate that. Honestly... I don't know. I'm already paying for the gym. I've got a free app that works for tracking. Adding another subscription feels...

**Maya:** *[Note: Subscription fatigue]* What if it was a one-time purchase?

**Sarah:** Maybe? I've bought workout programs before. Like, I bought a $60 powerlifting program once.

**Maya:** And did you finish it?

**Sarah:** *[Laughs]* No. I fell off after like week 4.

**Maya:** Because of the flexibility thing?

**Sarah:** Partly. Also just... life. But yeah, the program didn't care that I missed a week.

**Maya:** So you've paid for fitness stuff before, but it hasn't solved this problem.

**Sarah:** Right. And that makes me kind of skeptical, I guess? Like, I want to believe an app could solve this, but I've been burned before.

**Maya:** What would you need to see to believe it would actually work?

**Sarah:** I think... I'd want to see it work for someone like me. A testimonial from another nurse or shift worker. Someone whose life is as chaotic as mine.

**Maya:** *[Note: Social proof from similar users]* That makes sense. Would you try a free version?

**Sarah:** Definitely. If it was free, I'd try it tomorrow.

**Maya:** But you're not sure you'd pay for it.

**Sarah:** I'd want to see if it actually helps first. If I used it for a month and it actually reduced my skipped days? Then yeah, I'd probably pay.

**Maya:** What's "probably pay" look like? Like, what price range?

**Sarah:** Honestly? If it was more than like $5/month, I'd have to think hard about it. I know that sounds cheap, but I already pay for gym, for the occasional program... it adds up.

**Maya:** *[Note: Price sensitivity - $5/mo ceiling]* That's really helpful. Last question - who else do you know who has this problem?

**Sarah:** Oh, literally everyone I work with. Nurses, specifically. Our schedules are insane. I can think of five people off the top of my head.

**Maya:** Would any of them be willing to chat with me?

**Sarah:** Probably? I can ask. Let me DM you a couple names if they're interested.

**Maya:** That would be amazing. Thank you.

*[Wrap-up conversation, thanks, etc.]*

## Post-Call Reflection

**Summary:** Sarah is exactly the target user. The problem is real and painful for her. But willingness to pay is low - she's been burned by fitness purchases before and has subscription fatigue. She'd try something free but $5/mo might be her ceiling.

**Key quotes captured:**
- "Feel like I failed anyway"
- "Big red X next to my workout"
- "Intelligent flexibility"
- "I want permission to do less without feeling like I gave up"
- "I'd want to see it work for someone like me"

**Hypothesis check:**

*My hypothesis:* Busy professionals will pay for an adaptive workout app.

*After this conversation:* Partially challenged. The PROBLEM is validated - Sarah clearly has it and would use a solution. But willingness to pay is weak. She's skeptical and price-sensitive.

**Problem validation:** 5/5

**Willingness to pay:** 2/5

**Key insight:** The problem is real, but Sarah's been burned before. She won't pay upfront - she needs to experience value first. Free trial or freemium is probably necessary.

**Signals:**

*Strong signals (leaned in):*
- "If I had something that said 'here's what you CAN do today' I'd probably go"
- "I'd try it tomorrow" (if free)
- "I can think of five people off the top of my head"

*Weak signals (pulled back):*
- "$5/month might be my ceiling"
- "I've been burned before"
- "Another subscription feels..."

**New questions raised:**
- Is the nursing/shift-work segment more valuable than general "busy professionals"?
- Is freemium the only viable model for consumer fitness?
- Would B2B (selling to gyms, healthcare employers) be better?

**Follow-up:**
- Send Sarah a thank you message

- Check if nursing segment pattern continues

## What This Conversation Revealed

**The good:**
- Problem is VERY real for Sarah
- She's tried solutions and they failed
- Strong word-of-mouth potential (offered referrals)
- Clear articulation of what she wants ("intelligent flexibility")

**The concerning:**
- $5/month ceiling is tough for a SaaS business
- Subscription fatigue is real
- "Free first, pay if it works" = freemium pressure
- Previous purchase didn't solve problem = skepticism

**The question Maya is now asking:**
Is this a REAL business or a nice-to-have product that people won't pay enough for?
